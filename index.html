Weaving Heritage through Genealogy: Exploring the Lineage of Basket Makers

Techniques for weaving baskets are passed down from teacher to students and are often contained within a single family. In some instances, families own rights to specific designs and patterns. Makers can often identify a specific teacher through their work and can connect instructor and student based on the similarity in design.

Quill boxes are created with many different designs. Some are geometric and symmetrical, while others have stories woven on the side. If you would like to see a 3D model of the quill box, click the photo!

Yvonne Walker

A member of the Little Traverse Bay Band of Odawa, Yvonne Walker-Keshick is one of the most well-known quill box makers in the country. Yvonne began quill work in 1968, under the tutelage of Susan Shagonaby, and committed to quill work full time, year-round, work in 1980. As a prolific maker and teacher, Yvonne was award the Michigan Heritage Award in 1992 and the National Heritage Fellowship in 2014.

Yvonne is also mother and teacher to Arnold Walker, featured below.

Geometric Designs

Quill boxes are constructed from birch bark and porcupine quills, with a sweet grass trim. Geometric Designs are typically the first patterns that students learn and they can be simple or very intricate. To the left is a small quill box, about 2 inches in diameter woven by Yvonne. The only exposed birch bark on this piece is the block where Yvonne included her initials "YMW." Aside from the sweetgrass, twisted around the middle of the box, the design is created exclusively from quills.

Animals in Motion

Quill boxes were traditionally created as food storage and often included pictures of food to identify contents. Animals are also common images in quill work. In this piece, Yvonne had woven a rabbit in motion along the sides of the box. When rotated, it appears the rabbit is running.
